Open Science Toolbox: Leveraging Methodological Reforms to Accelerate Research

Rick Klein, Benedict Jones, Niamh Stack, Lisa DeBruine

Open Science Toolbox: Leveraging Methodological Reforms to Accelerate Research

Rick Klein, Benedict Jones, Niamh Stack, Lisa DeBruine

Abstract

Open science reforms may be seen as added barriers to research. This symposium emphasizes the opposite: ways to utilize these reforms to accelerate research programs. Included talks will discuss results from large-scale replication projects (‘Many Labs 2’), crowdsourcing science, reproducible and automatic results reporting, and how to teach open science.

Crowdsourcing Psychological Science

Benedict Jones

Crowdsourcing presents a solution to problems of low power and lack of diversity in research samples that often undermine conclusions from psychological research. I will focus on my lab’s experience conducting a preregistered study through the Psychological Science Accelerator, a globally distributed network of >350 laboratories from >45 countries.

Do Psychological Effects Vary across Sample and Setting? Findings from Many Labs 2

Richard Klein

This talk presents results from Many Labs 2, a recent large-scale project in which 28 psychological effects were replicated across 125 samples from 36 countries (N = 15,305). This talk will emphasize whether replication effect size varies based on the specific sample or setting of data collection.

Teaching Open Science

Niamh Stack

To address recent concerns about the reliability of psychological findings, some have emphasized the need to improve training at a grassroots level (cf. Button et al., 2016). This talk discusses how we can answer this call by supporting undergraduates in becoming more intelligent ‘consumers of science’ (Diener & Biwas-Diener, 2016).

Writing Reproducible Manuscripts

Lisa DeBruine

Perhaps half of published psychology papers contain at least one inconsistent statistic (e.g., Nuijten et al, 2016). I will discuss methods for reproducible manuscripts that reduce transcription errors and act as self-documentation of data and analysis. These techniques promote open science and make it easier to meet new journal requirements.
